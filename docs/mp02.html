Introduction

The American Time Use Survey (ATUS) is a joint effort between the US Bureau of Labor Statistics and the US Census Bureau to capture how Americans spend their time everyday. Over 20 years, Americans recorded what they did and in this mini-project I will analyze how that time was spent in order to find and compare time usage patterns across key demographic groups. I will explore the ATUS data using dplyr tools to combine and manipulate the data to extract important demographic data. I will analyze the data with complex analytic pipelines, which I will then summarize for a non-technical audience with data visualization.

In my final analysis, I will pose as a product manager for a time management app and create a market research article that applies this data and focuses on the time use patterns in three target demographics.

Data Acquisition and Preparation

Data Acquisition from ATUS

Data is acquired from ATUS resources websites, cleaned and combined into data frames that identify survey respondents, survey activities, their corresponding activity codes and important demographic information.

load_atus_data <- function(file_base = c("resp", "rost", "act", "cps")){
    if(!dir.exists(file.path("data", "mp02"))){
        dir.create(file.path("data", "mp02"), showWarnings=FALSE, recursive=TRUE)
    }
    
    file_base <- match.arg(file_base)
    
    file_name_out <- file.path("data", 
                               "mp02", 
                               glue("atus{file_base}_0324.dat"))
        
    if(!file.exists(file_name_out)){
        
        url_end <- glue("atus{file_base}-0324.zip")
        
        temp_zip <- tempfile(fileext=".zip")
        temp_dir <- tools::file_path_sans_ext(temp_zip)
        
        request("https://www.bls.gov") |>
            req_url_path("tus", "datafiles", url_end) |>
            req_headers(`User-Agent` = "Mozilla/5.0 (Macintosh; Intel Mac OS X 10.15; rv:149.0) Gecko/20100101 Firefox/149.0") |>
            req_perform(temp_zip)
    
        unzip(temp_zip, exdir = temp_dir)
        
        temp_file <- file.path(temp_dir, basename(file_name_out))
        
        file.copy(temp_file, file_name_out)
    }
    
    file <- read_csv(file_name_out, 
                     show_col_types = FALSE)
    
    switch(file_base, 
           #add cps data to determine if respondent is married
           cps=file |>
             filter(TULINENO == 1) |>
             mutate(marital_status = case_match(PEMARITL,
               1 ~ "Married - spouse present",
               2 ~ "Married - spouse absent",
               3 ~ "Widowed",
               4 ~ "Divorced",
               5 ~ "Separated",
               6 ~ "Never married"),
             #check if respondents are married
               has_spouse = marital_status %in% c("Married - spouse present", "Married - spouse absent"),
               is_retired = PEMLR == 5,
             #check if respondent works in education by main job occupation code
               in_education = PRDTOCC1 == 8)
             ,
           resp=file |> 
                  rename(survey_weight = TUFNWGTP, 
                         time_alone = TRTALONE, 
                         survey_year = TUYEAR,
                         survey_month = TUMONTH,
                         weekly_earnings = TRERNWA) |>
                  #add a column that counts number of children from roster file
                  left_join(
                    load_atus_data("rost") |>
                      filter(relation == "Self") |>
                      select(participant_id, age, sex), 
                    join_by(TUCASEID == participant_id)
                  ) |>
                  left_join(
                    load_atus_data("rost") |>
                      filter(age < 18) |>
                      count(participant_id, name = "n_children_under18"),
                    join_by(TUCASEID == participant_id)
                  ) |>
                  # pull in CPS data to add marital_status and has_spouse columns
                  left_join(
                    load_atus_data("cps"),
                    join_by(TUCASEID == participant_id)
                  ) |>
                  #coalesce will turn NA values into 0
                  mutate(n_children_under18 = coalesce(n_children_under18, 0L),
                         labor_force_status = case_match(TELFS,
                            1 ~ "Employed - at work",
                            2 ~ "Employed - absent",
                            3 ~ "Unemployed - on layoff",
                            4 ~ "Unemployed - looking",
                            5 ~ "Not in labor force"),
                         #creates column indicating enrollment
                         school_enrollment = case_match(TESCHENR,
                           1 ~ "Enrolled",
                           2 ~ "Not Enrolled",
                           -1 ~ "Blank"),
                         #create demographic variable that checks if enrolled and teschlvl matches college/university
                         college_enrolled = TESCHENR == 1 & TESCHLVL == 2)
           ,
           rost=file |>
                  mutate(sex = case_match(TESEX, 1 ~ "M", 2 ~ "F"),
                         #adding relation to respondent
                         relation = case_match(TERRP,
                           c(18,19) ~ "Self",
                           20 ~ "Spouse",
                           21 ~ "Unmarried partner",
                           22 ~ "Own household child",
                           23 ~ "Grandchild",
                           24 ~ "Parent",
                           25 ~ "Brother/sister",
                           26 ~ "Other related person",
                           27 ~ "Foster child",
                           28 ~ "Housemate/roommate",
                           29 ~ "Roomer/boarder",
                           30 ~ "Other nonrelative",
                           40 ~ "Own nonhousehold child < 18")) |>
                  rename(age = TEAGE), 
           act =file |>
                mutate(activity_n_of_day = TUACTIVITY_N, 
                       time_spent_min = TUACTDUR24, 
                       start_time = TUSTARTTIM,
                       stop_time = TUSTOPTIME, 
                       level1_code = paste0(TRTIER1P, "0000"), 
                       level2_code = paste0(TRTIER2P, "00"),
                       level3_code = TRCODEP,
                       #location of activity
                       location = case_match(TEWHERE,
                         1 ~ "Home",
                         2 ~ "Workplace",
                         3 ~ "Someone else's home",
                         4 ~ "Restaurant bar",
                         5 ~ "Place of worship",
                         6 ~ "Grocery store",
                         7 ~ "Other store/mall",
                         8 ~ "School",
                         9 ~ "Outdoors",
                         10 ~ "Library",
                         11 ~ "Other place",
                         12 ~ "Car, truck, or motorcycle (driver)",
                         13 ~ "Car, truck, or motorcycle (passenger)",
                         14 ~ "Walking",
                         15 ~ "Bus",
                         16 ~ "Subway/train",
                         17 ~ "Bicycle",
                         18 ~ "Boat/ferry",
                         19 ~ "Taxi/limousine service",
                         20 ~ "Airplane",
                         21 ~ "Other transportation",
                         30 ~ "Bank",
                         31 ~ "Gym/health club",
                         32 ~ "Post office",
                         89 ~ "Unspecificed place",
                         99 ~ "Unspecified transportation",
                         (-1) ~ "Blank"
                       )) 
           ) |> 
                rename(participant_id = TUCASEID) |>
                select(matches("[:lower:]", 
                               ignore.case=FALSE))
}

5. On average, how many hours do Americans with one or more children in the home spend taking care of their children (all activities including healthcare and involvement in their children’s education)?

Display Code 
avg_childcare_hours <- activities |>
  filter(
    level1_code == "030000",
    level2_code %in% c("030100", "030200", "030300")) |>
  group_by(participant_id) |>
  summarise(total_childcare_min = sum(time_spent_min)) |>

# Function to calculate typical levels and fraction hitting target
calc_typical <- function(demographic, activity_filter, targets) {
    activities_filtered <- demographic |>
        select(participant_id, survey_weight) |>
        left_join(activities_labeled, join_by(participant_id)) |>
        filter({{ activity_filter }}) |>
        mutate(time_spent_min = coalesce(time_spent_min, 0L),
          #if come across level1_code, will collapse all corresponding level2 tasks into one row
               task_level2 = if_else(level1_code == "020000", task_level1, task_level2)) |>
        filter(!is.na(task_level2)) |>
        group_by(participant_id, survey_weight, task_level1, task_level2) |>
        summarise(total_min = sum(time_spent_min), .groups = "drop")
    
    typical <- activities_filtered |>
        group_by(task_level1, task_level2) |>
        summarise(
            low    = wtd.quantile(total_min, weights = survey_weight, probs = 0.25),
            medium = wtd.quantile(total_min, weights = survey_weight, probs = 0.50),
            high   = wtd.quantile(total_min, weights = survey_weight, probs = 0.75),
            avg    = weighted.mean(total_min, survey_weight),
            .groups = "drop"
        ) |>
        left_join(targets, join_by(task_level2))
    
    target_hit <- activities_filtered |>
        left_join(targets, join_by(task_level2)) |>
        filter(!is.na(target)) |>
        mutate(hits_target = total_min >= target) |>
        group_by(task_level1, task_level2, target) |>
        summarise(pct_hitting_target = weighted.mean(hits_target, survey_weight),
                  .groups = "drop")
    
    list(typical = typical, target_hit = target_hit)
}

What have you been up to? A common question among friends, coworkers, and acquaintances that give us just a little bit of insight into who we are. Luckily, the Bureau of Labor Statistics and US Census Bureau has taken some of the mystery out of this guessing game with its American Time Use Survey (ATUS). With this treasure trove of time usage data from the past 20 years, we’re able to dig into our key demographic of users and their daily habits so that we can answer the core questions of our user research: Who are they?, What do they do?, and Are we engaging and realistic in our time goals for users?.

Who Are They?

Our core demographic thus far is made up of Stay at Home Dads, College Undergraduates and Working Mothers all with good reason to need a helping hand when it comes to making their time allocation work for their unique needs.

We broke down and defined these three groups as follows:

  • Stay at Home Dads are males with one or more children who reported their labor force status as ‘Not in the Labor Force’ and who are not of retirement age (below 65) or retirement status.

  • College undergraduates are individuals of all genders enrolled in college and between the ages 18-22. This captures the traditional “typical” first time undergraduate student not far removed from high school and still figuring out how to manage their time wisely.

  • Working Mothers are women with one or more children who reported a labor force status of ‘Employed’ and were not retirement age (below 65).

What Do They Do?

For each of these groups, we’ve identified key activities that take up a significant amount of time in these users’ days and that users would like to improve upon like hitting the gym for longer. To gauge how useful our application may be to these individuals, we analyzed how each of these groups have historically spent time on these activities on average and how they measure up to what we believe would be desired target times for these groups.

Among these groups, we see that our app may be most useful for working mothers since we’ve identified more key activities and responsibilities in general for this group. When comparing working mothers’ typical time usage against our key activity targets, we also identified the most opportunities for time improvement in activities such as caring for household children, health-related self care, and participating in sports/exercise.

Are We Engaging and Realistic in Our Time Goals?

Our target time levels are designed to be close enough that users won’t get discouraged by difficulty but far enough to ignite the fire of competition among their peers. When measuring up the fraction of users in each group who can hit their respective target time goals, we see that users in each group can easily achieve half or more of the intended time goals with two or three outstanding goals in each group. It’s our hope that this balance will keep the completionists among us engaged and the more casual users more than happy with their outcomes.

Undergraduates are the group with the least amount of activities where more than 50% of users can hit target levels, which may indicate difficulty keeping up with taking classes, doing homework and socializing.

Among activities that are shared across all demographic groups, working mothers spend on average less time on all activities while stay at home dads seem to have the luxury of time at hand. These two groups may seem somewhat similar at the outset with many overlapping activities but we see that working moms aren’t able to hit targets in participate in sports/exercise and care for household children even though their target thresholds are significantly lower than stay at home dads. Moreover, undergraduate students only slightly led in time spent socializing category and for the most part played second fiddle to stay at home dads, which makes sense since none of a student’s main priorities (going to class or studying) are present here.

These disparities highlight and suggest segmentation in prioritization of activities among these groups. Our approach is correct in using dynamic thresholds for target levels since each major group’s needs are clearly different. Working moms may need to do more with less time so they need lower targets in certain activities to hit target levels and get a sense of accomplishment out of our application. Stay at home dads are able to handle higher thresholds due to them having more time in the day not being taken up by work. Undergraduate students want to work hard in school and are not very concerned with housework.

Overall, our target demographics are well defined as a great starting point for application launch. Time usage analysis across these groups indicates we will have a varied audience of users with differing priorities that will require dynamic tiers of target thresholds in order to keep these group engaged, happy and challenged.
